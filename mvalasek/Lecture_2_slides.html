Logic

  • Informally, a discipline concerned with truth value of statements, relationships between objects, and inference based on them
  • There are several systems of logic but we will only discuss Boolean logic, in which a statement can be exclusively either TRUE (1) or FALSE (0)
  • The basis of all maths and computing
  • If you want to be able to code, you need to understand at least the basics
  • Note. The following is a strictly informal treatment of logic. Instead of using formal notation, I will use the R notation
  • Text in monospace on a brown background is R code: to get used to what it looks like

Logic: Truth value

  • Statement is a meaningful utterance about objects in the world and their relationships.
    • e.g., “Today is Wednesday.” or “Two is equal to five.”
  • Based on the nature of the relationships and the premises, a statement can either be TRUE (1) or FALSE (0)
  • Premises don’t have to be “true” in the sense of corresponding to our experience of reality. We can assess the truth value of a statement even if it’s based on outlandish premises

Logic: Truth value

  1. All rivers are laughter
  2. All laughter takes a chance
    Therefore
  3. All rivers take a chance
  • The conclusion is a valid inference based on the premises and so is TRUE, even though it sounds like New Age “wisdom”
  • R implements this

Logic: Boolean operators

  • Logical operators express relationships between objects (equality, ==, is an example)
  • There are lots of them but we will focus on four Boolean operators:
    • NOT (!)
    • AND (&)
    • OR (|)
    • exclusive OR, XOR (the xor() function in R)

Logic: Boolean operators

  • They are quite intuitive except for the OR/XOR distinction
  • A statement linking two objects statements with OR is TRUE if either of the partial statements is TRUE or both of them are TRUE
  • XOR returns TRUE if and only if one of the statements is TRUE

Probability

  • System of attributing chance to events
  • Based on a foundation of logic
  • Informs all of statistics
  • Main approaches: frequentist and Bayesian
  • We focus on the frequentist approach but be aware that Bayesian statistics has been gaining a lot of traction in recent years

It’s a tricky business

  • Unlike things we’ve talked about, probability is not intuitive
  • We didn’t evolve to solve probabilistic problems analytically but to adopt heuristics
  • The whole field started as a result of this counter-intuitive nature
    • One Antoine Gombaud, Chevalier de Méré noticed that when he bet on rolling at least 1 double-six in 24 rolls of two dice, he lost more often than when he bet on rolling at least 1 six in 4 rolls of a single die and was puzzled by it and challenged mathematicians to explain why
    • Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat took up the challenge and, between themselves, laid the groundwork of modern probability theory
  • RE M?r? – Boy must he have gambled a lot to notice that!

Definition

If there are \(a\) ways in which \(X\) can occur and \(b\) ways in which \(X\) can fail to occur, then the probability of \(X\) occurring is \(P(X) = a/(a+b)\)

  • So what’s the probability of rolling a 1 on a six-sided die?

Jargon

  • Let’s say we’re flipping a fair coin 10 times
  • Outcome is the possible result; either heads (H) or tails (T)
  • Event is a set of outcomes, e.g., H H T H T T H H T H
  • Experiment is the process generating simple events and producing outcome; flipping a coin
  • Sample space is the set of all possible outcomes; all combinations of Hs and Ts of size 10

Basic calculation

Basic calculation

Basic calculation

  • Given definition above, since there are 3 ways we can get exactly one T and 5 ways we can get some other number of Ts, the probability of getting exactly one T if we flip a fair coin three times is \(P(A) = 3 / (3 + 5)\)

Basic calculation

  • Similarly, there is only one way of getting HHT (event \(a\)) when flipping a fair coin three times – and therefore seven ways this can fail to occur – so the probability of this happening is \(P(a) = 1 / 8\)

Basic calculation

  • OK, your turn, what is the probability of a subset of \(A\) that does not include \(a\)?
  • \(P(A\ \&\ !a) = P(A) - P(a)\)
  • \(P(A\ \&\ !a) = .375 - .125 = .250\)

Conditional probability

  • Conditional probability \(P(A|B)\) is the probability of \(A\) occurring given that \(B\) occurred
  • Here, it’s the probability of getting exactly 1 T in 3 flips of a fair coin given H on the first flip
  • Note. ‘|’ is conditional probability, not the ‘OR’ operation

Conditional probability

  • To calculate \(P(A|B)\) applying the Bayes’ theorem, we need to know the probability of \(A\), the probability of \(B\), and the probability of \(B\) given \(A\)
  • \(P(A|B) = \frac{P(B\ \&\ A)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B|A)\times P(A)}{P(B)} = \frac{2/3\times 3/8}{1/2}\)

Conditional probability

  • To calculate \(P(A|B)\) applying the Bayes’ theorem, we need to know the probability of \(A\), the probability of \(B\), and the probability of \(B\) given \(A\)
  • \(P(A|B) = \frac{P(B\ \&\ A)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B|A)\times P(A)}{P(B)} = \frac{2/3\times 3/8}{1/2}\)

  • We want to know the prob of the intersection but only with respect to B, not to entire sample space

Independence

  • In probability theory, two events are said to be independent if the occurrence of one does not influence the probability of the other
  • We saw that \(P(HPV) \approx .015\) but \(P(HPV|pos) \approx .46\)
  • Likewise, \(P(pos) \approx .031\) but \(P(pos|HPV) = .92\)
  • That means that having HPV and testing positive for it are dependent events
  • Example of independent events would be getting H on first flip and getting T on second

Sampling

Why sample?

  • We want to say something about the world
  • We want to empirically assess that which we’re commenting on
  • Unfortunately, we don’t have access to every single instance of that thing
  • So we look at some instances, take measurements, quantify relationships of interest and make inferences about other, unobserved instances

Words. Beautiful words. The best words.

  • Population – all possible observations in the sample space
  • Sample – a subset of the population we actually collected data from
  • Parameter – a property of interest in the population
  • Statistic – a quantified expression of the property of interest in the sample
  • We use the statistic derived from the sample to make inferences about the population parameter

Random draw

  • When taking a sample from the population, we assume the process by which we sample is random
  • Every member of the population has the same probability of getting selected
  • That does not mean that every value has the same chance of being selected!
##  population - ten integers from 1 to 10
pop <- 1:10

##  pick 5 of them at random
sample(pop, 5)
[1]  7  6  2  3 10

##  different result each time
sample(pop, 5)
[1] 7 9 3 8 6
  • If I open a bag of Celebrations chocolates and randomly pick one, I may get any of the 8 treats
  • If I “sample” one from the same box the morning after the party, I’m virtually guaranteed to get a Caramel or a Bounty
  • Why is this? Let’s see…

Sampling from a finite population

  • There are two kinds of sampling
    • with replacement
    • without replacement
##  sampling WITH replacement
sample(pop, 10, replace = T)
 [1]  4  4  8  2  9  9  3  7  3 10

##  sampling WITHOUT replacement
sample(pop, 10)
 [1]  1  4  9  6  7  5  8  2  3 10

Sampling with replacement

  • Let’s say our box of CeleBraTiONs contains 10 of each of the 8 chocolates
  • Pick a chocolate at random, record which one it is, put it back, repeat
  • What’s the probability of picking a Malteaser on the first draw? On the second?
  • \(P(malt1) = 10/80\)
  • \(P(malt2) = 10/80\)
  • When sampling from a finite population with replacement, is any given draw influenced by the previous draws?
What do we call this property?

Sampling without replacement

  • Now pick a chocolate – it’s a Malteaser! – keep it, and pass the box on
  • What’s the probability of picking a Malteaser on the first draw? On the second?
  • \(P(malt1) = 10/80 = .125\)
  • \(P(malt2) = 9/79 = .114\)
  • When sampling from a finite population without replacement, individual draws are not independent!

Sampling in practice

  • When picking a sample, we assume that the population is infinite
  • Even though we sample without replacement, the individual draws are essentially independent

Variables

More lingo

  • When we collect a sample, it is because we want to measure certain attributes (e.g., age)
  • We collect one – or several – observations of the given attribute from each member of the sample
  • Domain is a set of all possible values an observation of a given attribute can take (0 - 122+ years)
  • Variable is set of attributes in which the observations vary across a domain
  • Something that’s a variable in one sample may not be a variable in another sample (first language in a sample of monolingual native English speakers is a constant, not a variable)

Types of variables

  • Several important ones:
    • Continuous – can take any value within the range of its domain (e.g., age)
    • Discrete – can take a specific set of values but not the values between these (e.g., number of rooms in a hotel)
    • Binomial – special case of discrete variable; can only take two values, usually 0 and 1 (e.g., pass/fail outcome of a test)

Levels of measurement

  • Nominal – categories with no relative ordering (e.g., study programme )
  • Ordinal – categories that can be meaningfully ordered (e.g., lecture quality ranking)
  • Interval – values are ordered and equidistant; no absolute zero point (e.g., IQ)
  • Ratio – like interval just with an absolute zero (e.g., age)

Variables in R

  • R has no way of knowing what level of measurement or type of variable is inside a vector
  • It can guess, but it’s much better if you tell it
  • logical values (TRUE/FALSE) can only be binomial and nominal
  • character values can only be nominal/ordinal and cannot be continuous
  • numeric values can represent any level of measurement and any type of variable
  • Read more here

Central tendency: Median

  • A special case of quantile
  • If you sort your data from the smallest value to the largest and divide into n equal parts, the quantile is the largest value in each of the parts
  • If n = 4, the individual cut-off points are quartiles
  • If n = 10, they are deciles
  • If n = 100, they are centiles (AKA percentiles)
  • Median is the midpoint, i.e., the only quantile if n = 2
  • Since we’re ordering, data must be at least ordinal
OR 2nd quartile, 5th decile, 50th percentile…

Central tendency: (Arithmetic)* Mean

  • What most people mean by average
    • population mean \(\mu\)
    • sample mean \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N x_i}{N}\)
  • If there are N observations of variable x in our sample, \(\sum_{i=1}^N x_i = x_1+x_2+x_3+\cdots +x_N\)
  • Requires at least interval-level data



* Other means are available (geometric, harmonic)

  • general rule of thumb
    • Greek letters – population parameters
    • Latin letters – sample statistics

Dispersion

  • Mode, median, and mean tell us about the central point in our data
  • They don’t tell us how spread the data are around this point
  • That is what measures of dispersion (spread) do
    • Range
    • Inter-quartile range
    • Deviation
    • Variance
    • Standard deviation

Dispersion: Range

  • Distance between smallest and largest value in sample
  • Drawback: Extremely sensitive to outliers
##  range() gives minimum and maximum
range(data$age)
[1] 23 33
##  get the distance
max(data$age) - min(data$age)
[1] 10

Dispersion: IQR

  • Inter-quartile range - distance between 1st and 3rd quartile
  • Drawback: Ignores half of the data

Dispersion: Deviation

  • Distance from every single value in the data from some convenient point
  • Mean is a convenient point
  • \(x_i - \bar{x}\), where \(x_i\) is every single data point
##  minus is a vectorised operation in R
data$age - mean(data$age)
[1] -3 -1  7  0 -1 -2

Dispersion: Deviation

  • There are as many deviations as data points
  • To get a single measure of spread, how about we add them all up?
  • Problem: More data points = more points to add up
  • BIG problem: They always add up to zero

Dispersion: Variance

  • How do we get around the BIG problem (deviations adding up to 0)?
  • By taking the square of the deviations
  • The sum of these is called the Sum of Squares
  • We can get around the problem by dividing the sum of squares by N
  • This is the variance

Dispersion: Variance

  • population variance \(\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}\)
  • sample variance, \(s^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N - 1}\)
    • reason for the \(N - 1\) is quite technical (see Bessel’s correction)
##  deviations
(dev <- data$age - mean(data$age))
[1] -3 -1  7  0 -1 -2
##  sums of squares
(ss <- sum(dev^2))
[1] 64
##  variance
ss / (length(data$age) - 1)
[1] 12.8
##  in-built function
var(data$age)
[1] 12.8

Dispersion: Standard deviation

  • Variance is a good measure of dispersion and is widely used
  • One minor inconvenience – measured in squared units
    • if age is measured in years, \(s^2_{age}\) is expressed in years2, whatever those are
  • Taking the square root of variance gives us a measure of spread in the original units
  • This is the standard deviation
    • \(\sigma\) for population
    • \(s\) (or \(SD\)) for sample

Dispersion: Standard deviation


\[s = \sqrt{s^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N - 1}}\]
